## Gokul's Threshold Check

Gokul Rajaram's original framework scores each moat as binary — "Maybe you assign one point to each mode they have" — and says "anything four or more, you're pretty damn secure. But if you have a two or three, it's a weak mode. And if you have one or less, you probably need to really build some more modes. If you have zero, you're screwed basically." For this scorecard, treat any moat scoring 5+ as "present" (the company meaningfully has this moat) and below 5 as "absent" (the company does not have this moat in a meaningful way). Gokul also acknowledges depth variation within a moat — "Zendesk is 0.5, NetSuite is a one" — which is why we use the 0-10 scale. Apply the test:

- List each moat that scores 5 or above (these count as "present")
- Count them
- **4+ moats present**: "Pretty damn secure" — PASSES
- **2-3 moats present**: Weak moat profile, vulnerable — like Salesforce and Atlassian at 3 each
- **0-1 moats present**: Critically exposed — "you're screwed basically"
- If the company is close (3 moats at 5+), note which moat could tip it over with execution

## Overall Durability Rating

Based on the total score and the threshold check, assign one of:

## Retention and Quality Indicators

Before assessing strengths and weaknesses, evaluate the company's retention metrics — Gokul considers these "the fundamental indicators of business quality":

- **Gross customer retention**: Are customers staying? High churn undermines every moat.
- **Net revenue retention (NRR)**: Is revenue from existing customers expanding? Gokul would "always take a company that's doing triple triple double double with excellent gross and excellent net revenue retention over a company that's growing 10x in a year with really bad customer retention and less than 90% NRR."
- **Retention under pressure**: Has the company maintained retention through competitive threats or seismic events? Untested retention should be taken "with a grain of salt."

State the company's known retention metrics and assess their strength. If unknown, flag explicitly.

## Access Product vs. Work Product Classification

Gokul distinguishes two types of products with different pricing and value dynamics:

- **Access products**: The value is in having access to the platform. Seat-based pricing works. (Example: ChatGPT Enterprise)
- **Work products**: The value is in the work output, not the number of users. Outcome-based pricing is appropriate. (Example: Harvey for legal contract processing)

## Commoditize-a-Complement Strategy Assessment

Gokul argues that strong companies must actively "commoditize a complement" — identify where the profit pool sits and make the adjacent layer free. Evaluate whether $ARGUMENTS is doing this:

- **Where is the profit pool?** Is it in the data or the workflows? The company must choose.
- **Is the company making the complement free?** If the profit pool is in workflows, data storage should be free (and pricing should shift to outcome-based). If the profit pool is in data, workflows should be given away.
- **Is the company at risk of BEING commoditized?** Are agentic companies building on top of them? Is the underlying platform (Shopify, Salesforce, AWS) likely to absorb their function?

## AI Disruption Risk Assessment

Based on the full moat profile, assess how vulnerable $ARGUMENTS is to AI-driven disruption — what Gokul calls "model intrusion." This is the risk that foundation model providers (OpenAI, Anthropic, Google) or AI-native startups eat into the company's market.

Consider:
- **Moats that protect against AI disruption**: Regulatory mode, physical infrastructure mode, deep workflow embedding, proprietary compounding data, and strong network effects all resist model intrusion.
- **Moats that do NOT protect against AI disruption**: Scale mode (in software — "everybody can produce software as cheaply as anybody else"), and distribution advantages that depend on fragile hacks rather than structural relationships.
- **Moats with mixed AI protection**: Ecosystem mode can be protective — Gokul's point is "you can vibe code an e-commerce hosting platform, no problem, but can you vibe code the hundreds of thousands of developers?" However, if the core platform gets disrupted, ecosystem partners may migrate to the new platform. Strong ecosystems resist AI disruption; weak ecosystems do not.
- **The thin wrapper test**: Is the company's core value something a foundation model could replicate with a thin wrapper? If yes, AI disruption risk is critical. Gokul's specific test: "Most bolt-on players are not doing it. They're simply using a GPT or Anthropic model and they're basically just adding a thin layer. You have to rebuild the entire experience end to end."
- **The vibe-code test**: Can a competitor "vibe code" the core product? Gokul uses this test explicitly: you can vibe code an e-commerce platform, but you cannot vibe code Shopify's ecosystem of hundreds of thousands of developers. You can vibe code restaurant access, but you cannot vibe code DoorDash's carrier density and reputation history. What parts of this company's value can and cannot be vibe-coded?

Assign an AI Disruption Risk level:
- **Low Risk**: Multiple moats that AI cannot easily replicate (regulatory, physical, deep workflow, proprietary data flywheel). Company is well-positioned even as AI capabilities improve.
- **Moderate Risk**: Some protective moats but meaningful exposure. AI could erode certain advantages while others hold.
- **High Risk**: Core value proposition is vulnerable to model intrusion. Limited protective moats. The company needs to move aggressively to build deeper defensibility.
- **Critical Risk**: Company is essentially a thin layer on top of capabilities that AI labs are building. Without rapid transformation, the business could be commoditized within 2-3 years.

State the risk level and justify in 3-4 sentences.

## Investor Lens: Durability Over Growth

Gokul's core investor philosophy is that durability matters more than growth rate. Apply his specific investor tests:

- **Durability over margins**: "My dear friend, margin is much less — it's not something I worry about. I think durability and defensibility is much more of a worry. Good companies, if they're defensible, they will have the ability to increase margin."
- **Pricing power potential**: Can the company raise prices over time? This matters more than current margins. PayPal raised prices 5 times in 3 years. The test is: "Do they have the potential? Do they have the ability to increase prices in the future?"
- **Go-forward IRR thinking**: At any valuation, ask whether the go-forward IRR justifies the position. "If your go-forward IRR at every liquidity opportunity is lower than what you think your fund should have, you should sell."
- **Non-consumption market**: Is the company creating a non-consumption market (like Shopify enabling anyone to sell, or Granola making people buy a separate notetaker)? These are "the biggest hits" but also "the biggest misses" if the bet doesn't play out.
- **Remarkable product test**: Is the product 10x or 100x better than the alternative? "If there is not a remarkable product, all the go-to-market distribution in the world will not save you."

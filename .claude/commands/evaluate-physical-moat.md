Gokul's framing is direct: "Wherever you have atoms it makes for a mode that's hard to displace. You can't — again I think humanoid robots will maybe at some point start taking — but it's probably a few years away." For pure software companies, he is blunt: "Physical infrastructure — software companies don't have any." Hyperscalers (AWS, Azure, GCP) are the prime example of companies that do — they have massive data centers that create true scale advantages. DoorDash has it through its logistics network. But for a pure software company, the absence of physical infrastructure is a vulnerability, not just a neutral factor. This moat is especially valuable because AI cannot easily replicate physical assets — it protects against "model intrusion" where AI labs try to enter adjacent markets. Companies that touch the physical world (logistics, hardware, manufacturing, real estate) have an advantage pure software companies fundamentally lack. The key insight for investors: "early stage" software companies explicitly "don't have any" of this moat, making it a dimension where honest scoring means most software companies get 0-2.

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Core product and whether it involves any physical-world operations
- Owned or operated physical assets (data centers, warehouses, logistics hubs, vehicles, hardware, real estate)
- Capital expenditure history and infrastructure investment trajectory
- Logistics, delivery, or physical service networks
- Competitive landscape and whether physical infrastructure is a differentiator
- Public statements from leadership about infrastructure strategy
- Geographic footprint and any regulatory ties to physical presence

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific physical assets, capital investments, or operational realities.

### 1. Owned or Operated Physical Assets
Does the company own or operate significant physical assets — data centers, logistics networks, hardware, manufacturing facilities, real estate, or fleet vehicles? Be specific about what these assets are and their scale. If the company is pure software with no physical assets, say so directly. Leasing cloud infrastructure from someone else does not count.

### 2. Capital Intensity of Replication
How capital-intensive would it be for a competitor to replicate these physical assets? Billions of dollars in data centers is a real barrier. A handful of leased office spaces is not. Quantify where possible — what has the company invested in physical infrastructure, and what would it cost a new entrant to match it?

### 3. Operational Advantages from Physical Infrastructure
Does the physical infrastructure create operational advantages — speed, reliability, cost efficiency, latency — that pure software cannot match? A company with its own data centers can optimize hardware for its workloads. A company with its own logistics network can guarantee delivery times. Identify the specific operational edge, or acknowledge that none exists.

### 4. Barrier to AI / Model Intrusion
Is the physical infrastructure a barrier to AI labs and model companies entering this market? Gokul specifically highlights that physical assets protect against model intrusion — OpenAI can build a better model, but it cannot build data centers or logistics networks overnight. Assess whether this company's physical assets would force an AI competitor to spend years and billions before competing.

### 5. Logistics, Delivery, or Physical Service Network
Does the company have a logistics, delivery, or physical service network that creates real-world operational scale? This includes driver networks, delivery fleets, warehouse operations, installation services, or field operations. These networks compound over time and are extremely difficult to replicate. If the company has no physical service layer, that is a clear gap.

### 6. Geographic and Regulatory Barriers
Are there geographic or regulatory barriers tied to the physical infrastructure? Permits, zoning, spectrum licenses, utility interconnections, international logistics agreements — these create durable advantages because they take years to secure and cannot be disrupted by better software. Identify any regulatory moats that reinforce the physical infrastructure, or note their absence.

## Physical Infrastructure Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No physical infrastructure mode. Pure software company with no owned physical assets. This is a vulnerability in Gokul's framework — the company has no protection against model intrusion on this dimension.
- **3-4**: Minimal physical infrastructure. Some physical presence but not a meaningful barrier to entry. Assets could be replicated with moderate capital.
- **5-6**: Meaningful physical infrastructure. The company operates real physical assets that create operational advantages, but they are not yet at a scale that constitutes a true moat.
- **7-8**: Strong physical infrastructure mode. Significant owned assets, high capital intensity for replication, and clear operational advantages that software alone cannot match. Real barrier to AI intrusion.
- **9-10**: Elite physical infrastructure mode. Massive, capital-intensive physical assets at global scale — think hyperscaler data centers or continent-spanning logistics networks. Would take a competitor billions of dollars and many years to replicate. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Most pure software companies should score 0-2 — do not inflate the score to be kind. Gokul is explicit that software companies simply do not have this moat, and that is a real weakness.

Evaluate a software company's "Network Mode" moat using Gokul Rajaram's framework from his interview with Harry Stebbings.

The company to evaluate is: $ARGUMENTS

Research this company thoroughly using web search, then produce a structured Network Mode evaluation based on Gokul Rajaram's framework. Gokul identifies network mode as one of the most powerful — and hardest to build — moats for software companies. Network mode is about network effects: the product gets more valuable as more users join. This is distinct from ecosystem mode (third-party builders creating integrations and add-ons) — network mode is specifically about user-to-user value creation. Facebook is the canonical example: Gokul worked there and saw firsthand how real identity plus real friends created unstoppable network effects that MySpace could never match because "on MySpace you didn't know who the people were." DoorDash demonstrated three-sided network effects across restaurants, drivers, and consumers. Faire built strong two-sided network effects connecting retailers and brands (Gokul invested at seed for $20M). Google's search improves as more queries refine its results. Genuine network effects take time to prove — for early-stage companies, it is often "too early to say." The key question is whether the product has DIRECT network effects (more users = better product for each user) or just indirect/marketplace dynamics. And with switching costs going to zero and data becoming portable, the network itself is what retains users — making true network effects even more critical.

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Core product mechanics and how users interact with each other on the platform
- User growth trajectory and engagement patterns over time
- Marketplace or multi-sided platform dynamics (if applicable)
- Data flywheel and how usage volume improves the product experience
- Competitive landscape and whether rivals can replicate the network
- Public statements from leadership about network effects and user growth strategy
- Evidence of organic, viral, or word-of-mouth growth driven by the network itself

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific numbers, user behavior patterns, competitive dynamics, or structural advantages.

### 1. Direct Network Effects
Does the product have direct network effects — does each additional user make the product inherently more valuable for every other user? This is the purest and most powerful form. Facebook had it: every friend who joined made the experience meaningfully better for everyone already there. Critically, Facebook beat MySpace because of real identity and real friends — "on MySpace you didn't know who the people were — they didn't have the real photo, they could come up with their thing." The identity layer created unstoppable network effects. Slack has it within organizations: each colleague on the platform makes it more useful for every other colleague. Figma's power was that "it was not just that a person could use it but it was much easier to share with other people in your company" — multiplayer dynamics that create defensive network effects. But many companies claim direct network effects that are actually just popularity or scale advantages. Be rigorous. Ask whether a user who joins today genuinely makes the experience better for users who joined yesterday, or whether the company just benefits from having more customers the way any business does.

### 2. Marketplace / Two-Sided Network Effects
Are there marketplace or two-sided (or multi-sided) network effects connecting supply and demand? DoorDash has three-sided effects: more restaurants attract more consumers, more consumers attract more drivers, more drivers enable faster delivery which attracts more consumers. Faire connects retailers and brands — Gokul invested at seed for $20M (the highest price YC deal at that point) because he saw genuine two-sided network effects, and it returned 100-200x. Assess whether the company operates a genuine marketplace where each side's participation makes the platform more valuable to the other side. Look for evidence that the cross-side pull is real and measurable, not just a theoretical story. A true marketplace network effect means the platform becomes the default meeting point for both sides.

### 3. Data Network Effects
Does the company have data network effects — does more usage generate more data, which feeds better algorithms or models, which creates a better experience, which attracts more usage? Google's search is the classic example: more queries improve search results for everyone. Assess whether the data flywheel is real and defensible. Many companies claim data network effects but the marginal value of additional data flattens quickly. The question is whether the company's data advantage is compounding or plateauing, and whether a competitor with sufficient capital could reach "good enough" data quality to neutralize the advantage.

### 4. Cross-Side Pull Strength
How strong is the cross-side pull — do users on one side actively attract users on the other? In the strongest network effects, users do the recruiting: people invite friends to join, sellers bring their buyers, creators bring their audiences. Assess whether the company benefits from organic cross-side pull or has to spend heavily on marketing and incentives to grow each side independently. If the company has to pay to acquire both sides separately, the network effect is weak regardless of what the pitch deck says. True cross-side pull means the network grows itself.

### 5. Tipping Point / Winner-Take-Most Dynamics
Is there a tipping point or winner-take-most dynamic in this market? Network effects markets often tip toward a single dominant player — once a network reaches critical mass, the gap becomes nearly impossible to close. Assess whether this market exhibits winner-take-most characteristics or whether multiple networks can coexist. Some markets (like ride-sharing) support duopolies; others (like social identity networks) tend toward monopoly. Also assess where the company is relative to the tipping point — has it already tipped in their favor, is it approaching the tipping point, or is the market still fragmented and contestable?

### 6. Defensibility Against a Well-Funded Competitor (The Vibe-Code Test)
How defensible are the network effects against a well-funded competitor starting from scratch? Gokul uses a specific test: "AI can vibe code the ability to access restaurants but it can't vibe code liquidity, carrier density, reputation history, all of those things. So marketplace density is a network effect which is structural." Apply this vibe-code test: can someone build the technology, but not the network? If a competitor with $1 billion could replicate the network within two years, the network effect is not a real moat. Assess the structural barriers: Would users have to simultaneously convince all their connections to switch? Is there a cold-start problem that makes bootstrapping a competing network extremely difficult? Or could a competitor target a niche, build density there, and expand outward? Also consider Gokul's point about switching costs going to zero — if data is portable and interoperable, does the network itself provide enough retention, or would users leave if something marginally better appeared?

## Network Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful network mode. The product does not get more valuable as more users join. Growth is driven by traditional sales and marketing, not network dynamics. The company might be a good business, but network effects are not part of its moat.
- **3-4**: Weak or nascent network effects. Some network-like dynamics exist — perhaps social features or community elements — but they are not core to the product's value proposition. Too early to say, or too thin to matter. Gokul notes that for early-stage companies, network effects are often "too early to say" — do not give credit for theoretical network effects that have not yet been proven.
- **5-6**: Moderate network effects with real but limited pull. The product does get somewhat better with more users, but the network effects are indirect, localized, or have not yet reached a tipping point. A well-funded competitor could plausibly challenge the network. The effects are real but not yet a durable moat.
- **7-8**: Strong network effects that are clearly visible and measurably improving the product. The company is at or past a tipping point in its core market. Cross-side pull is organic. A competitor starting from scratch would face a genuinely difficult cold-start problem. The network is a real moat, though it may have geographic or segment limitations.
- **9-10**: Elite network mode. The network is the product. Direct network effects are so strong that the platform is effectively unassailable in its core use case. Users cannot get the same value anywhere else because the people and interactions they need are only on this platform. Facebook-level: real identity, real relationships, self-reinforcing engagement. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding. A company that is "too early" for proven network effects should score low, not get credit for potential. Network effects are either demonstrably working or they are not.

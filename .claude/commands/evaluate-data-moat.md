Evaluate a software company's "Data Mode" moat using Gokul Rajaram's framework from his interview with Harry Stebbings.

The company to evaluate is: $ARGUMENTS

Research this company thoroughly using web search, then produce a structured Data Mode evaluation based on Gokul Rajaram's framework. Gokul argues that for pure software companies, data mode is one of only two moats you can truly hang your hat on (the other being workflow): "Data and workflow modes are the two things you're really hanging your hat on as a software investor." Brand is explicitly NOT a moat in his framework. Data portability is increasing and switching costs are trending toward zero — "the ability to port your data as a business or consumer from any ecosystem to another ecosystem is going to be very easy" — so the data asset must be truly proprietary and compounding, not just stored data. Companies that slap a thin AI layer on top of GPT or Anthropic models do not have real data mode. Gokul's canonical example: Spotify's Discover product uses "a decade of listening behavior across hundreds of billions of people. You can't create that Discover product easily." Atlassian has "unique proprietary data on all the code out there because it's being checked in" but "they need to use that data to build products" — having the data is not enough; you must build with it.

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Core product and how it collects and uses data
- Data infrastructure and any proprietary models or AI capabilities
- Competitive landscape and what data advantages it holds
- Customer lock-in dynamics and data portability
- Public statements from leadership about their data strategy
- Revenue model and whether data drives pricing power

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific product features, data types, or competitive dynamics.

### 1. Proprietary Data Asset
What proprietary data does the company collect that competitors cannot easily replicate? Is this data generated through the product itself, or is it commodity data that could be sourced elsewhere? Be specific about the exact data types and why they are hard to replicate.

### 2. Data Flywheel / Compounding
Does the data asset compound and improve with every user interaction? Describe the flywheel mechanism: more users lead to better data, which leads to a better product, which attracts more users. If the flywheel is weak or theoretical rather than proven, say so directly.

### 3. Model Building / Fine-Tuning
Is the company building or fine-tuning its own models on this proprietary data? Or is it wrapping third-party foundation models with a thin layer? Gokul's specific investor test: "Go deep into what is the data asset you're creating. Does it get better with time? Do I believe it gets better with time? Are you building your own model over time? Are you fine-tuning a model and improving it over time?" He also flags that "Most bolt-on players are not doing it. They're simply using a GPT or Anthropic model and they're basically just adding a thin layer." Companies must "tune the model for their customer base" — using Notion as a positive example where "the AI agents need to now get better based on how the user interacts with it." Be honest about where this company falls on that spectrum.

### 4. Data Portability Vulnerability
How vulnerable is the data moat to portability? Can customers easily export their data and switch to a competitor? Gokul emphasizes that switching costs are going to "essentially zero," so the data asset itself — not the switching cost — must be the moat. Assess whether the value lives in the raw data (portable) or in the network/compounding effects (not portable).

### 5. Differentiation vs. Commodity + AI Wrapper
Is the data truly differentiated, or is this commodity data with a thin AI layer on top? Many companies claim data advantages that are really just "we have a database and we added an LLM." Be rigorous here. What makes this data actually unique?

### 6. Pricing Power from Data
Does the data create real pricing power? Can the company charge more over time because the data asset makes the product meaningfully better than alternatives? Or is pricing under pressure because the data advantage is thin?

## Data Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful data mode. Commodity data or thin AI wrapper. Switching costs are the only retention mechanism.
- **3-4**: Emerging data asset but flywheel is unproven or slow. Data is partially replicable. The data exists but the company is not yet building with it effectively.
- **5-6**: Solid data asset with a working flywheel, but portability risk or competitive replication is a real concern. Calibration note: Gokul counted Atlassian as having data mode because of "unique proprietary data on all the code out there" but noted "they need to use that data to build products" — a company at the low end of this range has the data but is still proving it can build differentiated products from it.
- **7-8**: Strong data mode. Proprietary, compounding data with clear flywheel dynamics and meaningful model investment.
- **9-10**: Elite data mode. The data asset is deeply proprietary, compounds aggressively, powers fine-tuned models, and creates durable pricing power. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding.

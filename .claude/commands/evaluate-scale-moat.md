## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific numbers, cost structures, competitive dynamics, or structural advantages.

### 1. Cost Advantages from Operating at Scale
Does the company have cost advantages from operating at scale that competitors cannot easily match? This is the foundational question. Producing more must be genuinely cheaper per unit because you already produce a lot. A company with 10,000 customers should have meaningfully lower per-customer costs than a competitor with 100 customers — not just because of revenue leverage, but because of structural cost reductions in production, delivery, or operations. If the company's cost advantage comes primarily from spreading fixed software development costs across more customers, that is the OLD scale advantage that AI has destroyed. A startup with good AI tools can now build equivalent software at a fraction of the cost. Be ruthless about distinguishing real scale cost advantages from the illusion of scale that comes from simply being big.

### 2. Physical Infrastructure and Data Center Ownership
Does the company own physical infrastructure — data centers, logistics networks, warehouses, manufacturing facilities, or other physical assets — that creates genuine scale economics? Gokul is emphatic: hyperscalers with their own data centers are the ones who can confidently claim scale mode. Physical infrastructure has increasing returns to scale that software alone no longer does. Assess whether the company has made massive capital investments in physical assets that would be prohibitively expensive for a new entrant to replicate. If the company rents its infrastructure from AWS or Azure, it does not have this advantage — it is borrowing someone else's scale. Owning the physical layer is what separates real scale mode from the appearance of it.

### 3. AI Erosion of Software Scale Advantages
Has AI eroded the company's traditional software scale advantages? This is Gokul's most important and most provocative claim. If the company's scale advantage was historically rooted in having built a large, complex software platform that was expensive and time-consuming to replicate, that advantage may now be worth very little. AI coding tools, foundation models, and cheap compute mean a well-funded startup can build competitive software far faster and cheaper than ever before. Assess honestly: could a team of 50 engineers with modern AI tools build something that matches 80% of this company's product functionality within 18 months? If yes, the software scale moat is gone regardless of how large the company is. Salesforce is Gokul's example — massive scale, but a score of only 3 because software production costs have been equalized by AI.

### 4. Pricing Power from Scale
Does the company have pricing power that derives from its scale position? PayPal raised prices five times in three years because its scale gave it leverage — merchants could not easily switch away from a payment network processing that volume. Scale-driven pricing power means the company can increase prices without proportional customer loss because its scale position makes it essential or irreplaceable. This is different from pricing power that comes from switching costs or network effects — scale-driven pricing power comes specifically from the cost and operational advantages of being the largest player. Assess whether the company has demonstrated this kind of pricing power, or whether it faces price pressure because competitors can match its offerings at similar or lower cost.

### 5. Unit Economics Advantage from Scale
What is the company's unit economics advantage from scale — specifically, how much lower are its marginal costs compared to a competitor at one-tenth the scale? This dimension looks at the concrete economics: cost per transaction, cost per customer served, cost per unit produced, cost per API call, cost per delivery. Companies with genuine scale mode show dramatically better unit economics that get better over time as volume increases. Companies without real scale mode show flat or only modestly improving unit economics because their costs scale linearly with volume. Look for evidence of non-linear cost curves — costs that grow much slower than revenue or volume. If the company's cost structure scales roughly linearly, it does not have meaningful scale mode regardless of how large it is.

### 6. Scale Trajectory — Growing or Static
Is the scale advantage growing — does more production make future production cheaper — or is it static? The most powerful scale moats are dynamic: each unit produced makes the next unit cheaper, creating a virtuous cycle that widens the gap with competitors over time. Static scale advantages are those where the company achieved a cost position at some point but is no longer improving — the gap is fixed and competitors can slowly close it. Assess whether the company's scale economics are compounding or plateauing. Also consider whether the market is growing in a way that rewards the company's scale position or whether market shifts (like the move to AI) are resetting the scale game entirely. A scale advantage that was built over 20 years of software development and is now being neutralized by AI in 2 years is not a growing moat — it is a shrinking one.

## Scale Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful scale mode. The company may be large, but its size does not translate into cost advantages that competitors cannot match. A well-funded startup with AI tools could build a competitive product at comparable or lower cost. Being big is not the same as having scale mode.
- **3-4**: Weak scale mode. The company has some cost advantages from scale, but they are modest, eroding, or primarily rooted in legacy software complexity that AI is rapidly neutralizing. The company might have slight infrastructure or operational scale benefits but nothing that creates a durable cost moat. Note: Gokul says pure software companies like Salesforce do not have scale mode at all — they should score 0-2. A 3-4 implies at least some physical-world or infrastructure scale advantage beyond pure software.
- **5-6**: Moderate scale mode. The company has real cost advantages from scale that are partially rooted in physical operations, infrastructure, or other assets that AI cannot easily replicate. However, the advantages are not overwhelming — a well-capitalized competitor could plausibly close the gap within a few years. The scale is real but not yet a decisive moat.
- **7-8**: Strong scale mode. The company has significant cost advantages driven by physical infrastructure, massive operational scale, or proprietary compute resources. These advantages are structural and would take years and billions of dollars for a competitor to replicate. The company can demonstrably produce or deliver at lower cost than anyone else in its market, and this gap is widening.
- **9-10**: Elite scale mode. Hyperscaler-level or equivalent physical-world scale. The company's cost advantages from scale are so large and so deeply rooted in physical infrastructure and operational volume that replication is nearly impossible. The company sets the cost floor for its industry. Very few companies deserve this — think AWS, the major cloud providers, or physical-world operators with decades of infrastructure investment.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding. Remember that Gokul explicitly said Salesforce does NOT have scale mode at all — "A pure software company can't get that scale board" — despite Salesforce being one of the largest software companies in the world. Pure software companies should score 0-2 on scale. If the company's scale advantage is primarily in software, it should score low. AI has reset the scale game for software. Only physical and infrastructure scale still counts.

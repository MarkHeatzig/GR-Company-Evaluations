Evaluate a software company's "Workflow Mode" moat using Gokul Rajaram's framework from his interview with Harry Stebbings.

The company to evaluate is: $ARGUMENTS

Research this company thoroughly using web search, then produce a structured Workflow Mode evaluation based on Gokul Rajaram's framework. Gokul argues that for pure software companies, workflow is one of only two moats you can truly hang your hat on (the other being data). Workflow mode measures how deeply a product is embedded in the customer's day-to-day operations. Gokul is explicit that switching costs are trending to zero: "Over the next one or two years the ability to port your data as a business or consumer from any ecosystem to another ecosystem is going to be very easy and then people are going to be able to replicate almost pixel by pixel the experience you have with one product in a different product. You'll have clones popping up left, right, and center." This makes the depth of workflow embedding — not switching costs — the actual moat. Companies must either own the full stack of workflows or risk being commoditized. Bolt-on AI that just adds features does not count — you need to rebuild the entire experience end to end. The deepest form of workflow embedding is agentic workflows that replace human labor entirely. Brand is explicitly NOT a moat in his framework.

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Core product and how deeply it is embedded in customer daily operations
- System of record status and what canonical data the product owns
- Multi-product strategy and breadth of workflow coverage
- AI and automation capabilities, especially agentic workflows
- Competitive landscape and platform replication risk
- Public statements from leadership about product strategy and workflow depth
- Customer switching behavior and retention dynamics

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific product features, workflow integrations, or competitive dynamics.

### 1. Operational Embedding Depth
How deeply is the product embedded in the customer's daily operations? Could the customer function without it, or would losing it break core business processes? A product that is "nice to have" scores low. A product whose removal would halt operations scores high. Gokul uses a spectrum: "NetSuite is an ERP that runs your business, they have a much much deeper mode than say Zendesk which is a lighter workflow mode. So that is a mode — you can say it's one, maybe Zendesk is 0.5, NetSuite is a one." Also consider: is this an "access product" (value is in having access, seat-based pricing works) or a "work product" (value is in the work output, outcome-based pricing is appropriate)? Work products that replace human labor have deeper embedding than access products.

### 2. System of Record vs. Lightweight Layer
Is the company a true system of record, or is it a lightweight layer sitting on top of someone else's system of record? Products that own the canonical data and workflows score high. Products that read from and write to another platform's database score low.

### 3. Multi-Product / Full Stack Breadth
Has the company built a multi-product suite that covers the full stack of customer needs, or is it a single-function tool? Reference Gokul's example of ServiceTitan with 32 products — the goal is to be "the only product that the company uses." Evaluate how many distinct workflow surfaces the company covers. Gokul is emphatic: "You cannot be a single product company." And product #2 must "emanate very naturally" from product #1 — it cannot be a disconnected add-on. Also assess which products serve as "profit pool products" vs. "retentive products" (a key distinction from Square, where Square Capital didn't make money but was excellent for retention). For vertical companies specifically: "You've got to really own full stack. It's harder otherwise to be a 10 plus billion dollar company."

### 4. Agentic Workflow Depth
Is the company building agentic workflows that autonomously replace human labor, or does it still require humans in the loop for core tasks? Agentic workflows that take action, make decisions, and close loops without human intervention represent the deepest form of workflow embedding. Gokul's positive examples: Podium and Intercom both "burned the bridges and created completely new AI-native products" reaching $100M+ in agent revenue. The ambition should be to "be the only product that the company uses and replace as much of the digital labor as you can." Companies should be targeting BPO spend, unreplaced headcount, and eventually layoff budgets — not just software budgets. This is how TAMs expand from software spend to labor spend.

### 5. End-to-End AI Rebuild vs. Bolt-On
Has the company rebuilt the experience end to end with AI, or has it just bolted AI features onto an existing product? Gokul distinguishes between adding AI search to an existing product (an upgrade) versus building "search as an experience with new UX primitives" (a transformation). His concrete example: document processing — "You couldn't actually extract structured information from unstructured documents till about 6 or 9 months ago. Now you can reliably parse dense legal contracts. So your experience around documents needs to be fundamentally different. If you're just getting someone to upload a document in a flow, you need to instantly give them immediate insights from the document while they're uploading it." Companies must "re-evaluate every single interaction and say what has changed" with each model generation. Look for evidence of fundamentally new interaction models, not just AI-powered enhancements to old screens. Companies that fail to rebuild end-to-end will become "zombie companies" that "try to add AI features as a last resort, not succeed, and be sold to PE."

### 6. Platform Replication Risk and Commoditize-a-Complement
Could the underlying platform — Shopify, Salesforce, AWS, etc. — easily replicate what the company does? If the product is a feature that the platform could ship in a quarter, the workflow moat is thin. If the product represents years of domain-specific workflow logic that a platform would struggle to rebuild, the moat is thick. Also evaluate whether the company is executing a "commoditize a complement" strategy: is it identifying where the profit pool sits (data or workflows) and making the other layer free? Gokul says systems of record like Salesforce must choose — "if the profit pool is in the workflows, they need to make data storage free and change pricing to an outcome-based model. If the profit pool is in the data, they need to give away workflows for free." Companies that fail to commoditize their complement will be outmaneuvered by agentic startups building on top of them. Finally, assess whether the company is expanding from software spend into labor spend — "It's no longer SaaS. It's basically services. You're going after the services spend" — which dramatically expands the TAM.

## Workflow Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful workflow mode or very lightweight. The product is a tool that customers could replace without major disruption. Not a system of record. No agentic workflows. Easily replicated by a platform. Calibration: Gokul rates Zendesk as "0.5" on his binary scale — a "lighter workflow mode" — which maps to roughly a 2-3 on this 10-point scale.
- **3-4**: Some workflow embedding but shallow. The product handles a single function and is not the system of record. Customers could switch with moderate effort. AI capabilities are bolt-on, not transformative. Calibration: Gokul says Monday.com "probably has a score of one" (out of 8 moats total) with workflow mode being its only moat — and even that is shallow compared to deeper systems of record.
- **5-6**: Solid workflow embedding with real operational depth. The product is embedded in daily operations and may be the system of record for its domain, but gaps remain in multi-product breadth, agentic capabilities, or platform replication risk.
- **7-8**: Strong workflow mode. Deeply embedded in customer operations, owns the system of record, covers multiple workflow surfaces, and is building meaningful agentic capabilities. A platform would struggle to replicate the domain-specific logic. Operational embedding depth and platform replication risk should carry the most weight in justifying this score. Calibration: Gokul rates NetSuite as a full "one" on his binary scale — "an ERP that runs your business" — which maps to roughly a 7-8 on this 10-point scale.
- **9-10**: Elite workflow mode. The product is so deeply embedded that removing it would break customer operations. Full multi-product suite covering the entire workflow stack. Agentic workflows are replacing human labor. End-to-end AI rebuild, not bolt-on. No platform could replicate this without years of domain investment. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding.

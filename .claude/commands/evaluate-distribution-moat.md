Research this company thoroughly using web search, then produce a structured Distribution Mode evaluation based on Gokul Rajaram's framework. Gokul identifies distribution mode as having "proprietary exclusive distribution" — not brand, not marketing spend, but proprietary channels and platform relationships that give the company an unfair advantage in customer acquisition. Brand is explicitly NOT a moat in his framework — "I think brand is no longer a strong mode. I explicitly excluded brand." Early-stage distribution hacks (viral tricks, growth hacks) are fragile and do not constitute real distribution mode — "early stage it's too hard to know what a distribution mode is unless you have some hack and these hacks never really stand the test of time." True distribution mode is durable, structural, and compounds over time. Gokul's canonical example is Intuit/QuickBooks: "You've trained a network of CPAs to only use QuickBooks... What a great distribution mode. Very hard to displace them." Other examples: Google's deal with Apple for default search, and Klaviyo's relationship with Shopify (though its strength depends on "how strong and tight" the preferred status is). Facebook taught Gokul "the power of distribution" — "Mark Zuckerberg is probably the best distribution genius in the world." Multiplayer products inherently have stronger distribution: "Most software products are single player and as soon as you make them multiplayer there is a uniqueness in switching, distribution etc."

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Core product and primary customer acquisition channels
- Platform partnerships, integrations, and default/preferred status arrangements
- Pricing strategy and whether a free or low-cost wedge product drives adoption
- Multi-product expansion and cross-sell dynamics
- Competitive landscape and how distribution advantages compare to rivals
- Whether the company created a new market or serves non-consumption (customers who never bought this category before)
- Public statements from leadership about go-to-market and distribution strategy

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific partnerships, platform relationships, or structural advantages.

### 1. Proprietary Distribution Channels
Does the company have proprietary distribution channels that competitors cannot easily replicate? This includes platform defaults, exclusive partnerships, embedded integrations, trained professional networks, or being the preferred/promoted option within another ecosystem. Being listed in a marketplace is not proprietary distribution — being the default or promoted option is. Gokul's strongest example: Intuit/QuickBooks trained an entire network of CPAs to only use QuickBooks — "My accountant said, 'No, I'm sorry. I don't use Xero. You shut it down.' I had to cancel Xero and go to QuickBooks." That is proprietary distribution through a professional network. Klaviyo's relationship with Shopify is another example, though Gokul notes the strength depends on whether "Shopify is actually going to promote them as a preferred product." Also evaluate whether the product is multiplayer — Gokul learned from Facebook and Figma that multiplayer products have inherent distribution advantages because "multiple people can use and it increases defensibility."

### 2. Wedge Product / Low-Cost Hook
Does the company have a wedge product — something free or very low cost that pulls customers into a broader ecosystem? Gokul says when selling to non-wealthy customers: "You need a wedge product that is almost cheap or free... Robinhood obviously offered free stock trading for a long time and that was their core pitch and that allowed them to basically get a lot of people. You need to have something free or some hook that is really low cost that allows you to expand." The wedge must be genuinely compelling on its own, not just a free trial. But note Gokul's warning: mass-market businesses with low ARPU "need millions if not tens of millions or hundreds of millions of people" — contrast with Palantir (fewer than 1,000 customers paying $20-100M each) or Veeva (went public with 4 customers). Assess whether the wedge creates a natural path to paid expansion and multi-product adoption, or whether it attracts users who never convert.

### 3. Durability vs. Fragile Hack
Is the distribution advantage structural and durable, or is it a fragile hack that will not stand the test of time? Gokul is explicit: early-stage distribution hacks — viral loops, referral bonuses, SEO tricks — rarely last. Real distribution mode is built on deep platform relationships, contractual arrangements, or network effects that strengthen over time. If the company's distribution depends on a single tactic that competitors can copy in a quarter, it is a hack, not a moat.

### 4. Platform Default / Preferred Status
Is the company the default or preferred option within another platform's ecosystem? This is the strongest form of distribution mode. Google pays Apple billions to be the default search engine on Safari. Klaviyo is promoted within Shopify. Being embedded as the default means customers arrive pre-sold — they have to actively choose to switch away. Assess whether the company holds this position and how defensible the arrangement is.

### 5. Non-Consumption Market Creation
Has the company created a non-consumption market — reaching customers who never bought this category of product before? Gokul highlights this as the source of both the biggest hits and biggest misses. Shopify is his biggest market-sizing miss: "I remember seeing Shopify at a billion and I was like how many e-commerce merchants are there really? And the TAM felt really constrained. What I missed was that Shopify was not just selling e-commerce. It was basically allowing anybody to sell." Other non-consumption examples: Uber (people who never used car services), Granola (AI meeting notes for people who never took meeting notes — "I would never have actually bought a notetaker before"), Gamma (presentations for people who never made decks), and Google and Facebook as "non-consumption markets — new behaviors that didn't exist before." Creating your own market is a form of distribution mode because you define the channel and there is no incumbent to displace. But note Gokul's warning: "If the bet doesn't play out, they're screwed. The bet plays out, they could be bigger than anything else."

### 6. Multi-Product Distribution Compounding
Does the company have a multi-product strategy that creates compounding distribution advantages? Gokul is direct: "You cannot be a single product company." Each additional product creates a new surface for customer acquisition and cross-sell. Evaluate whether the company's product portfolio creates distribution leverage — does selling product A make it easier to distribute product B? Or is each product sold independently with no compounding effect?

## Distribution Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful distribution mode. Customer acquisition relies on paid marketing, sales teams, or fragile growth hacks. No structural advantage in reaching customers.
- **3-4**: Some distribution advantage but it is narrow, unproven, or easily replicable. May have a wedge product but no platform lock-in or default status.
- **5-6**: Solid distribution mode with one or two real structural advantages — perhaps a meaningful platform partnership or a working wedge-to-expansion funnel — but gaps remain in durability or multi-product compounding.
- **7-8**: Strong distribution mode. Proprietary channels, durable platform relationships, and evidence of compounding distribution through multi-product expansion. Competitors face a real structural disadvantage in reaching the same customers.
- **9-10**: Elite distribution mode. The company is the default within a major platform ecosystem, has created its own market category, and compounds distribution across multiple products. Displacing this company requires breaking entrenched structural relationships, not just building a better product. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding. Remember: brand awareness is not distribution mode, paid marketing spend is not distribution mode, and early-stage viral hacks are not distribution mode.

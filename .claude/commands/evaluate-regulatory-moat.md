Evaluate a software company's "Regulatory Mode" moat using Gokul Rajaram's framework from his interview with Harry Stebbings.

The company to evaluate is: $ARGUMENTS

Research this company thoroughly using web search, then produce a structured Regulatory Mode evaluation based on Gokul Rajaram's framework. Gokul argues that regulatory moats are among the most durable in software because they are structural — you cannot bypass a license requirement with a better algorithm. Companies operating in regulated industries (finance, healthcare, legal, government) benefit from barriers that competitors need years and significant capital to overcome. Regulatory moats are especially valuable in the age of AI because they protect against "model intrusion" — the risk of AI labs eating your business. Unlike brand (which Gokul explicitly says is no longer a strong moat), regulatory barriers do not erode when a foundation model gets better. Fintech is a prime example: "If you're moving money, you're generally in a good place. So anything that touches money we feel there's a very strong mode there, much more defensible." Gokul's canonical example is Coinbase, where he sits on the board: "They have MTLs, money transmission licenses, state by state. They registered with FinCEN, all of those things. It makes it impossible for a company to use anybody else but Coinbase to custody their crypto because of that reason." Companies like Veeva thrive precisely because heavily regulated industries create enormous barriers to entry — Veeva "went public with four customers." Multi-year procurement contracts also constitute regulatory-adjacent moats.

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Core product and the regulatory environment it operates in
- Licenses, certifications, and compliance frameworks it holds or enables
- Competitive landscape and what regulatory barriers protect it
- Customer base and whether customers are in regulated industries
- Public statements from leadership about compliance, regulation, or licensing strategy
- History of regulatory changes in its industry and how they affected the company
- Whether the company helps customers meet their own compliance obligations

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific regulations, licenses, certifications, or compliance frameworks by name.

### 1. Regulated Industry Positioning
Does the company operate in a regulated industry (finance, healthcare, legal, government, energy, etc.)? How central is regulation to the industry's structure? A company selling project management software to banks is adjacent to regulation. A company that processes payments or stores patient health records is directly inside the regulatory perimeter. Assess whether the company is truly subject to regulation or merely sells to regulated customers. Gokul's framing for assessing model intrusion risk: "If you have some of the other modes which are physical and so on, it becomes easier. If you're a pure software company, which of those apply to you? I think fintech is a good one." Fintech and money-movement businesses are the strongest regulatory positioning for pure software companies.

### 2. Licenses, Certifications, and Compliance Frameworks
What specific licenses, certifications, or compliance frameworks does the company hold that competitors would need years to obtain? Examples include SOC 2 Type II, HIPAA compliance, PCI DSS, FedRAMP authorization, state money transmitter licenses, broker-dealer registration, FDA clearance, etc. Be specific — name the actual certifications. Assess how long and how expensive it would be for a new entrant to obtain the same credentials. If the certifications are table-stakes (e.g., SOC 2 for any SaaS company), score accordingly.

### 3. Regulatory Barriers to Entry and Switching Costs
Does regulation create meaningful barriers to entry for competitors or switching costs for customers? When a customer is using the company's product to meet a compliance obligation, switching introduces regulatory risk — the new vendor must also be compliant, and migration itself may require audit. Assess whether customers are locked in by regulation (not just by habit or convenience) and whether a new entrant would face years of regulatory groundwork before competing.

### 4. Resistance to AI Disruption (Model Intrusion Protection)
How resistant is this regulatory advantage to AI disruption? Gokul specifically values regulatory moats because AI labs cannot simply build a better model to bypass licensing requirements, audit obligations, or legal frameworks. A company whose moat depends on holding a money transmitter license in 50 states is protected regardless of how good GPT-N gets. A company whose moat depends on having better compliance analytics is more vulnerable. Assess whether the regulatory moat is structural (AI cannot route around it) or analytical (AI could replicate the value).

### 5. Compliance Workflow Embedding
Does the company help its customers meet their own compliance requirements, making the company itself part of the regulatory workflow? This is the intersection of regulatory moat and workflow moat — the company becomes embedded not just in operations but in the customer's legal and regulatory obligations. If removing the product would put the customer out of compliance, the moat is exceptionally deep. Assess whether the company is a compliance enabler or merely a compliant vendor.

### 6. Regulatory Trajectory
Is the regulatory environment getting stricter (expanding the moat) or loosening (shrinking it)? New regulations, increased enforcement, and expanding compliance mandates all strengthen a regulatory moat. Deregulation, regulatory rollbacks, or shifts toward self-regulation weaken it. Assess the direction of travel in the company's regulatory environment over the next 3-5 years.

## Regulatory Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful regulatory moat. The company operates in an unregulated or lightly regulated space. Certifications held are table-stakes. Regulation neither protects the company nor creates switching costs.
- **3-4**: Modest regulatory positioning. The company sells to regulated customers or holds some certifications, but the regulatory barriers are not central to its defensibility. A well-funded competitor could achieve parity within a year.
- **5-6**: Solid regulatory moat. The company operates within a regulated perimeter, holds meaningful certifications, and benefits from real barriers to entry. However, the moat may be partially vulnerable to AI disruption or regulatory loosening.
- **7-8**: Strong regulatory moat. The company holds hard-to-obtain licenses or certifications, is embedded in customer compliance workflows, and the regulatory environment is stable or tightening. AI cannot easily route around the barriers.
- **9-10**: Elite regulatory moat. The company is deeply inside a heavily regulated industry, holds licenses that take years and millions to obtain, is a critical part of customer compliance infrastructure, and the regulatory trajectory is expanding the moat. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding.

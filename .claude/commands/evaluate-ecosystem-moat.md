Evaluate a software company's "Ecosystem Mode" moat using Gokul Rajaram's framework from his interview with Harry Stebbings.

The company to evaluate is: $ARGUMENTS

Research this company thoroughly using web search, then produce a structured Ecosystem Mode evaluation based on Gokul Rajaram's framework. Gokul identifies ecosystem mode as one of the key moats for software companies: "If you have a platform or ecosystem where many third parties have built on and rely on, you have a mode." Ecosystem mode is about having a rich ecosystem of third-party integrations, developers, partners, and add-ons built around your platform — not about user-to-user network effects (that is network mode). Gokul's canonical example is Shopify: "You can vibe code an e-commerce hosting platform, no problem. But can you vibe code the hundreds of thousands of developers and third parties who've built all these applications on Shopify? Every Shopify merchant I know uses like at least five or six other third party apps. That's a huge part of the Shopify ecosystem." Salesforce also has ecosystem mode with thousands of apps and consulting partners. The ecosystem creates lock-in because customers don't just use your product — they use your product plus integrations plus custom workflows built by partners. But ecosystem takes years to build (at early stage "it's too early to say"), and if the core platform gets disrupted by AI or a better alternative, ecosystem partners may migrate to the new platform.

## Research Phase

Use web search to gather information about the company's:
- Platform and API strategy, including developer documentation and SDKs
- Third-party app marketplace or integration directory (size, growth, quality)
- Developer community and ecosystem (number of developers, activity levels, investment programs)
- Consulting, implementation, and systems integrator partner network
- Customer adoption of integrations and partner-built solutions
- Public statements from leadership about their platform and ecosystem strategy
- Competitive landscape and how the ecosystem compares to rivals

## Evaluation Dimensions

Analyze the company across each of these six dimensions. For each one, provide a concrete, evidence-based assessment — not generic platitudes. Cite specific numbers, partner names, marketplace stats, or competitive dynamics.

### 1. Third-Party Developer / Partner Ecosystem Size and Vibrancy
How large and vibrant is the third-party developer and partner ecosystem? Is there a thriving community of independent developers building on the platform, or is the ecosystem thin and dependent on a few big partners? Look for concrete signals: number of active developers, frequency of new app releases, developer conference attendance, quality of developer documentation, and whether developers are making real money on the platform. A healthy ecosystem is one where third parties are economically motivated to keep building.

### 2. Integration, App, and Add-On Depth
How many integrations, apps, or add-ons are built on the platform? More importantly, how many are actively maintained and widely adopted? A marketplace with 5,000 apps where 50 matter is very different from one with 500 apps where 200 are essential. Assess the depth and quality of the catalog, not just the headline count. Are these integrations solving real customer problems, or are they shallow connectors that Zapier could replace?

### 3. Consulting / Implementation Partner Network
Is there a consulting and implementation partner network — the equivalent of Salesforce's massive SI ecosystem? This layer matters enormously because it creates an entire industry of professionals whose livelihoods depend on the platform. Certified consultants, implementation partners, and training programs all deepen ecosystem lock-in. If a company has thousands of certified professionals in the market, ripping out that platform means ripping out an entire services supply chain.

### 4. Customer-Built Custom Workflows and Switching Cost Amplification
Do customers build custom workflows, integrations, and automations on top of the platform that dramatically increase switching costs? This is where ecosystem mode compounds: it is not just the vendor's product, it is the customer's own investment in configuring, integrating, and extending it. Assess how deeply customers embed the platform into their operations. The deeper the customization, the higher the switching cost — but only if those customizations are platform-specific and not portable.

### 5. Self-Reinforcing Ecosystem Dynamics
Is the ecosystem self-reinforcing? The strongest ecosystem moats have a flywheel: more partners build on the platform, which makes the platform more valuable to customers, which attracts more customers, which attracts more partners. Assess whether this flywheel is actually spinning or is merely theoretical. Look for evidence of acceleration — is the ecosystem growing faster over time, or has it plateaued? Are new categories of partners emerging, or is growth coming from the same segments?

### 6. Platform Migration Vulnerability and Commoditize-a-Complement Risk
How vulnerable is the ecosystem to platform migration if a better core alternative emerges? Gokul flags this as a real risk: if AI or a new entrant disrupts the core product, ecosystem partners may migrate to the new platform. Assess whether the ecosystem is loyal to the platform or loyal to the customers. If partners can easily rebuild their integrations on a competing platform, the ecosystem moat is thinner than it appears. Also critically assess the "commoditize a complement" dynamic from both directions: Is the platform commoditizing its ecosystem partners (making their integrations free to drive stickiness), or are agentic startups building on top of the platform and threatening to commoditize it? Gokul warns that systems of record "can't just wait around for other people to build on top of them" — they must actively decide where the profit pool is and commoditize the adjacent layer.

## Ecosystem Mode Score

After the dimensional analysis, assign a single score from 0 to 10:

- **0-2**: No meaningful ecosystem mode. Few third-party integrations, no developer community, no partner network. The product stands alone.
- **3-4**: Early ecosystem forming. Some integrations and partners exist, but the ecosystem is thin, fragile, or dependent on a handful of players. Too early to call it a moat.
- **5-6**: Solid ecosystem with real breadth. Meaningful number of integrations and partners, customers are building on the platform, but the flywheel is not yet dominant or the ecosystem is vulnerable to migration. Calibration: Gokul counts Atlassian as having ecosystem mode — "there's a lot of third party things that are built around them" — placing it in this range or above.
- **7-8**: Strong ecosystem mode. Large, active developer and partner community. Customers are deeply embedded with custom workflows and multiple integrations. Self-reinforcing dynamics are clearly visible.
- **9-10**: Elite ecosystem mode. The platform is an industry standard with a massive, self-sustaining ecosystem of developers, partners, consultants, and customer-built customizations. Displacing it would require migrating an entire economic ecosystem, not just switching a product. Very few companies deserve this.

Present the score prominently with a clear, specific justification tied back to the six dimensions. Do not grade on a curve or be generous — Gokul's framework is deliberately demanding. A company that is "too early" for ecosystem mode should score low, not get credit for potential.
